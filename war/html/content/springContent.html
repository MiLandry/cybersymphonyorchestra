Spring is nothing more than somewhere your program can go to get an object with all of its information populated

According to StackOverflow, TutorialsPoint, Wikipedia, a plethora of other tutorial and learn-it-yourself sites, as well as a number of your fellow developers and colleagues will all parrot the same thing when talking about Spring:

"It's a framework."

"It's an Inversion of Control Container."

"It achieves Loose Coupling."

"It uses Dependency Injection."

While most of these things are well and true, that is not what Spring is about. That doesn't explain its success and popularity. To me, Spring is nothing more than somewhere your program can go to get an object with all of its information populated. That is to say, it is an implementation of the Factory Pattern.

I hate describing Spring in any other way; Spring solves a problem that is programming-specific, it can sound pretentious to IT professionals outside of development, and early coders will be overwhelmed with confusing jargon. For taking the first step in understanding Spring, all you need to know is that you can go ask spring to make you an object with all its dependencies already populated. Knowing that Spring implements the Factory Design pattern is much more useful to the user than understanding that dependencies are injected into beans. Mentioning Dependency Injection is confusing because that is the implementation of the factory, not the purpose or reason of Spring. Prospective Spring users are more likely to comprehend the need of some way to fetch pre-populated objects, rather than putting a framework that features an Inversion of Control container because concepts like Inversion of Control and Dependency Injection are solutions to problems that these users don't have; these concepts are the solutions to Spring's problems.

And I am not really sure why people mention 'loose coupling' along with spring. Spring does not "achieve" Loose Coupling, programming to interfaces achieves loose coupling. Spring just saves you time when you actually join up your interfaces with your implementations.

If we are going to attract more minds to the coding profession, we have to shore up (and, at times, correct) how we communicate our concepts of development.
